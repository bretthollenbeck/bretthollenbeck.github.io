# The Impact of Dollar Store Expansion on Local Market Structure and Food Access
El Hadi Caoui, Brett Hollenbeck, Matthew Osborne, Elina T. Page

*Quantitative Marketing and Economics*, Accepted.

# Introduction

The growth of large chain retailers over the past several decades has transformed the retail sector in the United States, with implications for competition, labor markets, and aggregate outcomes such as inflation and productivity. A more recent and less studied phenomenon is the broad and dramatic rise of the dollar store retail format. Following previous waves of growth by large retail chains, the three main dollar store chains (Dollar General, Dollar Tree, and Family Dollar) have, in many ways, become the dominant mode of retailer in many markets, impacting competition, affordability, convenience, and food accessibility for a significant portion of the population.

The distinguishing features of these chains are the use of single or limited price points, particularly selling most goods for \$1, and assortments consisting of small serving-size basic consumables, clearance or irregular goods, and notably, a lack of fresh produce. Beginning in the 1950s, these chains exhibited slow but steady growth over the decades that followed, establishing themselves primarily in small towns in rural areas. Following the 2008 recession, the growth of this format has been and continues to be exceptionally rapid.[^1] In 2021, there were more of these stores operating than all the Walmarts, CVS, Walgreens, and Targets combined by a large margin. During the period 2018-2021, roughly half of all retail stores that opened in the U.S. were dollar stores. In volume terms, the dollar store segment is still quite small relative to Walmart and other large retail chains. However, the magnitude of the number of individual dollar stores and their spatial distribution has raised a number of policy issues.

In particular, many policymakers have expressed concerns that the rapid entry of dollar store chains in their cities has forced out local independent retailers, including neighborhood grocery stores. The latter may be especially concerning to the extent it reduces access to produce and other perishable food items for low-income residents, creating "food deserts." These concerns and others have led many localities to ban dollar store chains from entering or pass dispersal regulations limiting their store density.[^2]

Broadly speaking, the arguments for and against dollar store chains fall along these lines. Proponents argue that they introduce additional choice into underserved retail markets and that their strategy of entering in low-rent areas and opening multiple stores in the same market results in greater convenience for customers who can make short trips for specific items rather than long trips to the nearest big box store, which could be a large distance away.

Opponents maintain that the aggressive entry strategy of dollar store chains has caused the exit of local independent retailers and prevented the entry of other retailers. Even if consumers value the convenience of the dollar stores, their strategy of offering basic consumables and household products efficiently using a low fixed cost model and avoiding the costly and complex provision of perishable food results in them capturing a large share of nearby grocers' profits. This could leave grocers unprofitable on the basis of food sales alone and potentially lead to exit, reducing convenient access to fresh produce and exacerbating nutritional deficits. Yet despite the extensive public and policy debate surrounding these issues, the academic literature has produced only limited evidence on these claims or the broader effects of dollar store chain expansion.

In this paper, we provide new empirical evidence on the core issues in this policy debate. Our primary research questions are the following: what has been the effect of dollar store expansion on consumers and their spending patterns, particularly spending on fresh or perishable food? Next, what has been the effect on local retail markets, and particularly on the number of independent grocery stores? For each of these questions, we both estimate average effects and use our granular data to analyze distributional impacts.

We leverage data from several sources. We track the number and type of retail stores, including dollar stores, across the U.S. using the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) Retailer panel, a yearly panel of SNAP-authorized retailers from 2008 to 2019. An advantage of this dataset is that it covers small independent retail stores, which are typically absent from other retail censuses used in the literature. We match the data on store openings and closings to the Circana (previously named IRI) Consumer Network panel, which contains household-level data on all retail purchases for a large nationwide sample of consumers.

We first use an event study design to study the effects of dollar store entries on consumer shopping behavior, using household fixed effects and time-varying demographics to capture consumer characteristics. We find that when dollar stores enter nearby, households shift a share of their purchases away from grocery and convenience stores to the dollar stores. Households also decrease their spending (and volumes purchased) on produce by modest but significant amounts (4% to 7.3%), an effect that is increasing in the number of dollar store entries. This average effect masks important heterogeneity across households: households with annual income less than \$45,000 and households with high travel costs (proxied by vehicle access) experience large declines in fresh produce spending (around $15\%$) whereas households with annual income above \$$70,000$ are not affected. This finding is consistent with the notion that, in low-income areas, consumers rely more on independent stores for their food purchases, because high travel costs limit their access to large chain grocers ([@ellickson_grieco_jue_2013]).

The estimated reduction in fresh produce spending due to dollar store entry explains between 13% and 27% of the difference in fresh produce spending between households with annual income below \$45,000 versus above \$$70,000$.[^3] By contrast, we do not find evidence that dollar stores lead to significant changes in spending on other food categories (e.g., canned produce, dairy, meats).[^4]

Next, we study the impact on local market structure. We control for market-level demographics and demographic trends, as well as market-year fixed effects, and consistently find that dollar store entries are associated with a significant decrease in the number of independent grocery stores. We emphasize that the impacted firms are generally small "mom and pop" grocery stores and not large chain supermarkets.[^5] The effect size is roughly the loss of one grocery store for every three dollar stores when measured in the area 0-2 miles around the entry location. In the area 2 to 5 miles away, the effect size becomes substantially smaller, suggesting the effects are local. The previous literature on the market structure effects of retail chain expansion has shown that big box chains such as Walmart do not compete intensely with local "mom and pop" grocery stores due both to vertical differentiation and their spatial distribution ([@ellickson_grieco_jue_2013]). Our results suggest that because of dollar stores' vertical positioning and spatial pattern of entry, their entry, by contrast, has led to large negative effects on these grocery stores.

Finally, we put the two sets of results together and study the relationship between market structure and consumer spending changes. The changes in consumption we observe could be driven by supply-side changes in food accessibility or by the fact that low-income budget-constrained households value the predictable prices and small package sizes of dollar stores over their limited assortment, e.g. lack of produce [@brecko2025dimensions]. We find that the reductions in fresh produce purchases following dollar store entry are not merely due to shifting shopping habits, but are caused in part by grocery store exits. While we are not able to fully evaluate whether consumers as a whole, or particular segments, are on net harmed by the expansion of dollar stores and the corresponding exit of independent grocery stores, our results are suggestive that some are and, moreover, speak directly to the concerns around "food deserts" raised by some policymakers. In addition, we contribute to our understanding of the nature of food deserts, and the topic of food access more broadly, by showing how changes in market structure can change consumer purchase habits in an important category.

**Related Literature.** This paper contributes to three lines of research in economics.

First, this paper is related to the study of consumers' grocery shopping behavior and food accessibility. There is an extensive literature studying nutritional inequality in the U.S., with studies focusing on price-, access-, and nutrition education-based interventions ([@levi_foodaccess_2]). Studies of food access have focused on introduction of grocery stores to markets designated as "food deserts,\" with case studies around individual store entries having found mixed results ([@rose_etal_2004], [@cummins_foodaccess_05], [@Weatherspoon_etal_2016], [@liese_etal_2014], [@cummins_foodaccess_14], [@elbel_etal_2015], [@dubowitz_etal_2015], [@verploeg_etal_2016]). Notably, [@Allcott2019] study a large number of grocery store and supermarket entries and find they have only small effects on the nutrition of nearby consumers and that nutritional inequality in the U.S. is largely explained by demand factors rather than limited food access, with differences in access and prices, explaining only about 10% of nutritional inequality. [@levi_foodaccess_1] find that access to grocery stores impacts fruit and vegetable spending by affecting shopping frequency, but only among households with a low value of nutrition and at distances of less than 1 mile. [@hristakeva_levine_hurricanes] leverage hurricane-induced temporary grocery store closures to detect supply-side effects and find that households affected by these closures shift the location and nutritional value of their purchases for a substantial period of time even after the grocery store has reopened. [@Byrne2022] study the effect of SNAP participation by retailers on consumers' shopping behavior and participating stores' inventory and revenue. They find that new SNAP retailers have a limited inventory of foods (e.g., no fresh produce) and do not significantly impact SNAP-eligible households' nutritional choices or prices paid in the 6 months following adoption.

We contribute to this literature by studying large numbers of dollar store and grocery store entry events, as well as large numbers of grocery store exits, to measure the impact of these events on households' shopping behavior, including spending on produce. We find results consistent with previous research (e.g., [@Allcott2019]) that entry of a grocery store or supermarket has a limited impact on shopping behavior and nutritional choices, but we depart from this literature in that we find that the exit of existing grocery stores and the entry of large numbers of dollar stores do have significant impacts on food purchases. If consumers' shopping behavior is characterized by inertia or habit persistence, as suggested by the store choice literature in marketing ([@bell_ho_tang]), this could generate asymmetric effects between grocery store openings and closings. Our results are therefore consistent with other work (e.g. [@griffith_nevo_aer]) finding that the supply side has a meaningful impact on nutritional outcomes. We also show how these effects can vary substantially based on household income.

Second, we contribute to the literature that studies the evolution of the U.S. discount retail sector. This literature has focused on the impact of big box retailers (e.g., Walmart, K-Mart) and the supercenter format on market structure and competition ([@jia_ecta], [@Zhu2009], [@baskernoel09], [@igami_2011], [@ellickson_grieco_jue_2013], [@grieco_2014], [@Arcidiacono2020]), on labor markets ([@Basker2005]), and the role of chain and density economies ([@holmes], [@houghtonellickson]). A key finding in this literature is that Walmart's entry primarily harmed larger chain retailers in the local area within two miles of entry ([@ellickson_grieco_jue_2013]). Small local retailers were not substantially harmed due to travel costs and horizontal and vertical differentiation between firms. Our results suggest that, whereas small local retailers were not in direct competition with big-box retailers like Walmart, the dollar store format is more of a direct competitor to these stores and has had a large impact on local retail markets as a result. The rise of the dollar store format, therefore, has a distinctive impact on retail and raises unique policy questions.

Our findings also contribute to the recent and emergent literature studying the dollar store format. [@feng_page_cash_ajph] show that, despite their limited food assortments, dollar stores represent a growing share of food purchases, especially in small markets. [@Chenarides2021] find that dollar stores are more likely to enter in low income and low food access areas, and that areas with dollar stores are more likely to remain without access to a supermarket; [@Marchesi2023] show that dollar store entry is associated with grocery store exit, lower retail employment and sales, with these effects being larger in rural communities. In concurrent work, [@Chenarides2023] study the impact of dollar store expansion using a dynamic model of entry and data from Texas. They find that dollar stores benefit supermarkets by displacing the competitive fringe of small independent retailers. In subsequent work, [@Schneier2023] investigates the effects of the first dollar store entry in a zipcode on prices paid and basket size. [@Cao2022] estimates a welfare model of dollar stores' entry via changes in retail variety and supply of private-label products, finding welfare gains from dollar store entry primarily via the introduction of dollar stores' private label products into consumer choice sets. [@grigsby_calage_ml] use machine learning methods to better understand how major dollar store chains make location decisions, conditional on demographic characteristics of neighborhoods.

Concurrent work by [@caouihollenbeckosborne] model a dynamic oligopoly game of spatial competition between dollar stores and rival retailers. Estimation of this game for small and rural markets reveals that dollar stores have lower operating and entry costs than rival retailers. Counterfactual simulations of entry regulation policies (e.g., an entry tax or dispersal policy) indicates that accounting for rival stores' spatial reallocation is crucial to quantify the net impact of dollar stores.

Finally, a recent working paper by [@cao_chevalier_handburg_parsley_williams] use cellphone location tracking data from 2019 to show that low-income consumers have a high revealed preference for making general merchandise purchases at dollar stores, and that the large expansion of dollar stores therefore provides welfare benefits to these consumers. Their analysis of choices focuses on surplus from trips to general merchandise stores and excludes grocery stores from analysis. Therefore, they do not address the potential welfare effects of grocery store exits or the impact of dollar store expansion on grocery stores. Relative to these other works, our primary contributions are to show in detail how dollar store entry impacts household fresh produce consumption, especially for low-income households with higher travel costs, to show using a broad national panel that dollar stores cause significant amounts of grocery store exit, and to connect these pieces and show that the changes in household consumption are partly driven by exits of nearby grocery stores.

Section [3](#sec:rf_market_structure) presents reduced-form static and dynamic event study results for the impact of dollar store entry on consumers' shopping behavior and local market structure. Section [4](#sec:conclusion) concludes.

# Industry Background, Data, and Descriptive Statistics {#sec:data}

In this section, we describe the history and nature of the dollar store chains, present our data sources, and provide some descriptive statistics on the industry.

Dollar General originated the dollar store concept in 1955, selling a wide selection of low-cost basic goods at a single \$1 price point. The format became popular and a number of competing variety retailers adopted it, including Family Dollar, founded in 1959. Through decades of steady growth and consolidation among competing chains, by the 2000s there remained three major dollar store chains: Dollar General, Family Dollar, and Dollar Tree. These chains distinguish themselves from other retailers by offering low prices in the form of a single price point or a limited number of round number price points.

Unlike other discount retailers like Aldi, they do not achieve their discounts by offering small selections and a large share of private labels. Instead, they offer moderately sized selections and a mix of major brand products and private labels.[^6] The stores are built in the 8,000-12,000 sq ft range and carry 10,000-12,000 SKUs. They also save costs by employing few employees and offering a minimal assortment of perishable foods. They primarily sell basic consumables in small package sizes, seasonal products, and irregular or outdated products off-loaded by major brands. Another distinguishing feature is their market entry strategy, with a focus on small and low income markets under-served by big box retailers.

The dollar store chains have grown rapidly over the past several decades, particularly so after the recession of 2008. By 2021, Family Dollar operated roughly 7,100 stores, Dollar General operated 18,000 stores, and Dollar Tree owned 4,350 stores. The combined nearly 30,000 stores are substantially more than the number of Wal-Marts (5,300 stores), Targets (1,900 stores), CVS (9,900 stores), and Walgreens (9,300 stores) combined and is significantly larger than the number of Subway restaurants (21,000 restaurants), the largest U.S. restaurant chain and is similar to the number of Starbucks locations worldwide (33,000). The three chains earned a combined \$47 billion in revenue in 2019. In 2015, the two smaller chains, Dollar Tree and Family Dollar, merged citing several potential complementarities between the two businesses: e.g., targeting broader ranges of customers, optimizing their combined real estate portfolio, exploiting synergies in sourcing, procurement, and distribution networks.[^7]

#### Data:

We combine several data sources to study dollar store expansion and the effects on consumers and local market structure.

The first is the SNAP Retailer panel, a yearly panel of SNAP-authorized retailers from 2008 to 2019. This dataset contains information on over 400,000 U.S. retailers including their store name and type. The SNAP retailer panel contains any store that accepts SNAP benefits. In addition to dollar stores, this includes convenience stores, combination stores (stores selling a combination of general merchandise and food products), grocery stores, drugstores, gas stations, supermarkets, and supercenters. We use the store type variable provided in the SNAP Retailer panel (rather than the store name) to classify stores by type (e.g., dollar, grocery, convenience store).

We extract the chain affiliation of each store as follows. The raw data contain store names that include store identifiers, format descriptors, and other extraneous information (e.g., "Dollar General 12700," "Walmart Supercenter 6789," "Walmart S/C 785," "Walgreens 07693"). To recover the underlying chain name, we standardize store names by lowercasing, removing numbers, and stripping common descriptors and formatting artifacts. We identify chains by grouping stores that share the same cleaned name and, in addition, by matching cleaned store names to a reference list of 140 major retail chains using exact substring and fuzzy string-distance methods.[^8] Stores that do not match any chain are classified as independents. Because store names are self-reported and lack standardization, this classification is inherently imperfect; some chain stores may be misclassified as independents. For all major chains in our reference list, we validate our store counts against figures disclosed in annual reports and find no significant discrepancies.

Table [1](#tab_SNAPretailers_count) shows store counts by type in the SNAP panel over 2010-2019, as well as the share of stores that are part of a retail chain. The USDA defines grocery stores as food-focused retailers that carry all four staple food categories and whose primary stock consists of food items.[^9] Supermarkets are larger establishments that carry all four staple food categories and feature ten or more checkout lanes with registers, bar code scanners, and conveyor belts. The category super store encompasses a broader set of very large retailers, including big-box stores, supercenters, food and drug combination stores, and warehouse retail-wholesale hybrids. These definitions are consistent with the second column of Table [1](#tab_SNAPretailers_count) in that grocery stores are independently owned, while supermarkets and super stores are predominantly operated by retail chains.[^10]

The SNAP retailer data is novel in the economics literature but has been used in the geography literature studying retail proximity (e.g., [@Shannon2018]) and is also used in recent work by [@cao_chevalier_handburg_parsley_williams]. The primary benefits of this public data source are that it is an annual measure and contains nearly the full universe of retailers in this industry. Crucially for this study, the panel includes small independent stores, which are typically absent from other retail census data used in the literature.

A potential concern with this dataset is that entry into the SNAP program may not necessarily indicate the start of operation of a physical store. In particular, as the SNAP program debuted in 2008, there may have been delays in stores joining the program for the first few years. We alleviate this concern in two ways. For chains, we can compare store counts in the SNAP panel against publicly disclosed store counts in chains' annual reports to investors. We do not find any significant discrepancies between the two sources. For independent stores, this approach is not possible: instead, we drop the first few years in the sample and restrict our analysis to the period from $2010$ to $2019$.

Overall, we believe that the SNAP data is ideal for answering our research questions for the following reasons. First, the administrative process for SNAP authorization was streamlined: applications are accepted on a rolling basis and typically processed within 45 days ([@Byrne2024]). Second, participation in SNAP by grocery stores is near-universal in our sample period.[^11] The economic incentives to join are also strong: SNAP accounts for 5--10% of sales for typical grocery retailers---and as much as 20% in low-income areas ([@PYMNTS2023HungerCliff]). Finally, we note that the concern discussed above does not affect exit events, as stores disappear from the SNAP panel when they cease operations. Also, since SNAP enrollment involves very low ongoing costs, stores are likely to remain enrolled once they join and as long as they are operating.

We also compile data from the Circana Consumer Network panel.[^12] The data contain household-level panels on all retail purchases for a nationwide sample of consumers.[^13] Throughout the paper, we use the "static\" panel in the Circana data, defined as households that meet a spending threshold by household size ([@Sweitzer2017]). In addition, the consumer panel includes detailed household location information at the census-tract level, which is more precise than other scanner datasets (e.g. Nielsen Homescan provides the household's zipcode). This allows us to conduct our analysis at a more granular geographic level and construct more precise measures of retail proximity. Although the household scanner data is standard in the IO literature, we include informative summary statistics of spending by retail channel and food category in [5](#tab:desc_stats_homescan) of the Appendix.[^14] These statistics show that spending at dollar stores is relatively low compared to other retail channels (e.g., supermarkets, supercenters). The largest expenditure share at dollar stores is for soda, snacks, candy, and crackers; whereas fresh produce spending is close to zero.[^15]

Finally, we collect market-level data on demographic characteristics from the Census and ACS at the census tract level. This allows us to study how market characteristics and consumer demographics affect dollar stores and other retailers' entry behavior and profits.

Second, to isolate preferences that are not merely due to dollar store proximity, we identify households with high dollar store spending who have no dollar stores within 2 miles. All else equal, these households are willing to travel longer distances to shop at this store format and may, therefore, benefit from entry as it reduces their travel costs. To implement this comparison, we select the sample of households with no dollar stores within 2 miles and regress their dollar store spending per year on household demographics (age, income, education, race, household size, marital status, occupation, and weekly hours worked), their retail environment (number of stores by format within 2 miles and from 2 to 5 miles of the household), and the survey year. Controlling for the retail environment is important because low-income households with no dollar stores within 2 miles may also lack access to other retailers (grocery and supermarkets).

We find that the main dimensions of heterogeneity are race and household income. Figure [2](#fig:winners_byincome_race) shows the estimated coefficients for income and race. Low-income households and black households have significantly higher spending at dollar stores, even when such stores are located far from the household. These patterns are consistent with the findings of [@cao_chevalier_handburg_parsley_williams] based on revealed preference to travel to particular store formats.

# Impact of Dollar Store Entry on Market Structure and Consumer Purchases {#sec:rf_market_structure}

## Effects on Consumers' Shopping Behavior {#sec:rf_consumer_spending}

This section studies how dollar store entry affects households' shopping behavior. As in [@Allcott2019], the identification strategy exploits variation in local supply within households. While retailers' entry location choices are endogenous and depend on household characteristics ([@cao_chevalier_handburg_parsley_williams]), we exploit the panel nature of the household scanner data to control for persistent household unobserved heterogeneity. In this context, it seems unlikely that retailers strategically time their openings to coincide with sudden shifts in a particular household's demand.

We use our data containing the annual universe of retailers and study the period between 2010 and 2019. During this time period, we observe 14,554 dollar store chain entries. For each entry location, we construct distance bands around each location using radii of 0-2mi, 2-5mi, and 5-10mi and locate households within these radii.

Our identification strategy for measuring the effects of dollar store chain entry on these outcomes is to use household fixed effects to account for unobserved time-invariant characteristics as well as time-varying household characteristics and year fixed effects to account for general time trends. We effectively compare households with nearby dollar store entries to those without.

We begin by analyzing how dollar store entries affect aggregate food spending. Next, we study spending and trip diversion from the grocery to the dollar store retail channel. Finally, we examine how these changes translate into spending on various food categories and the nutritional value of households' shopping baskets. We aim attention at spending and volume of purchases of fresh produce, specifically, because access to this particular food group has been at the center of the dollar store policy debate.

In this section, the unit of analysis is the household-year. We consider both static and dynamic (event study design) specifications. Let $DS_{bct}$ denote the number of dollar store entries that have occurred within distance band $b$ (e.g., 0-2mi, 2-5mi) from census tract $c$ by period $t$. Let $\mathbf{X}_{it}$ denote time-varying household characteristics (e.g., age, education, employment, marital status). Let $Y_{ict}$ denote the outcome variable for household $i$ living in census tract $c$ in period $t$. We consider the following static specification

$$
\label{eq:rf_consumer_static}
        Y_{ict} = \delta DS_{bct} + \beta \mathbf{X}_{it} + \gamma_i + \eta_t + \epsilon_{ict}

$$

where $\gamma_i$ and $\eta_t$ are household and time fixed effects.

As the effects we estimate are likely persistent and heterogeneous over time, we also consider a dynamic specification in an event study framework. Let $E_{bct}$ denote a dummy for whether a dollar store entry has occurred within distance band $b$ (e.g., 0-2mi, 2-5mi) from census tract $c$ by period $t$. We consider the following dynamic specification

$$
\label{eq:rf_consumer_dynamic}
       Y_{ict} = \sum_{\tau}\delta_{\tau} E_{bc,t-\tau} + \beta \mathbf{X}_{it} + \gamma_i + \eta_t + \epsilon_{ict}

$$

where $E_{lt}$ denote a dummy for whether a dollar store entry has occurred in location $l$ by period $t$. This differs from the specification in Equation ([\[eq:rf_consumer_static\]](#eq:rf_consumer_static)) in that the coefficients on dollar store entry are subscripted by $\tau$, the difference in years measured relative to the entry date. This allows for both dynamic policy effects, such as a delay in the effect on local households as habits change, and for detecting the presence of pre-trends in grocery store activity prior to dollar store entry. For this analysis, we also focus only on a binary dummy for whether or not an entry occurs, rather than the number of entries.

This design is essentially a Difference-in-Difference with a staggered roll-out of treatment. This type of empirical design has been shown to have the potential for biased results ([@GOODMANBACON2021254], [@SUN2021175]). Consequently, we present results based on heterogeneity-robust estimators, e.g., [@CALLAWAY2021200].[^18] We plot the results in the manner suggested by [@Freyaldenhoven_et_al_event_study], with confidence intervals adjusted for multiple hypothesis testing.

**Spending and number of trips by retail channel.**

[\[table:reg_static_aggspent_anyds_0_2\]](#table:reg_static_aggspent_anyds_0_2) shows the effect of dollar store entry (within 2 miles of the household) on spending and the number of yearly trips by retail channel.[^19] The results indicate that dollar stores divert spending away primarily from the grocery and convenience retail channels, but not from supercenters or club stores. Entry is associated with an increased number of trips to the dollar channel, from $4.8$ to $5.5$ trips following one entry and $5.8$ following two entries. The impact of entry on the number of grocery and convenience trips is negative and significant.



**Spending on fresh produce and dollar store entry.**

Next, we turn to the effect on fresh produce spending, a key outcome of interest for policymakers.[^20] [\[table:reg_frshprod_anyds_0_5\]](#table:reg_frshprod_anyds_0_5) and Figure [3](#fig:event_plot_frshprod_anyds) show the effect of dollar store entry within 2mi and from 2 to 5mi of the household on fresh produce spending. Overall, we find a statistically significant negative effect of entry within 2mi of the household on produce spending across samples (all households in the "static\" panel, those part of a balanced panel, and those part of a balanced panel and reporting random weight (unpackaged produce) products). The magnitude of the effect increases with the number of dollar store entries but is overall modest in size, with 2 or 3 dollar store entries causing a decrease in produce spending of roughly \$7 or 4.5% of pre-entry spending.[^21]

[\[table:reg_frshprod_anyds_0_5\]](#table:reg_frshprod_anyds_0_5) shows that these effects are localized spatially. Entry of dollar stores in the 2-5mi distance band (columns (4) to (6)) does not significantly affect produce spending. [3](#fig:event_plot_frshprod_anyds) shows the corresponding event study plots for the balanced sample, using a two-way fixed effect as well as a heterogeneity-robust estimator ([@CALLAWAY2021200]). Consistent with the static analysis, we find that the effects are localized and negative only at the 0-2mi range. Moreover, the negative impact on fresh produce spending is dynamic, with the magnitude increasing in the time since entry.

**Spending on other food categories.**

One argument in favor of dollar store entry is its potential for lowering prices and increasing cost savings for consumers. On the whole, these findings suggest a modest but significant impact on consumers' dietary choices. This masks important heterogeneity across consumers: households with income below \$$45,000$ (bottom tercile of the income distribution) and households with high travel costs experience large declines in fresh produce spending whereas higher-income households are not affected.

While previous research (e.g., [@Allcott2019]) has shown that entry of grocery stores and supermarkets has economically small effects on healthy grocery shopping, the analysis above demonstrates that dollar store entry in close proximity is associated with a moderate but significant reduction in spending on and volumes of fresh produce. This effect could result either from direct effects of proximity to dollar stores or could result from dollar store entry causing grocery store exit. Next, we investigate the impact of dollar store entry on local market structure and especially on grocery stores.

## Effects on Market Structure {#sec:rf_market_structure_sub}

In this section, we present evidence on the impact of dollar store chain entry on local retail market structure. Our goal is to evaluate whether or not dollar store chain entry leads to decreases in the number of local grocery retailers.

As before, for each entry location, we construct distance bands around each location using radii of 0-2mi, 2-5mi, and 5-10mi. Our main outcome of interest is the number of independent grocery stores within these radii.

Our identification strategy for measuring the effects of dollar store chain entry on these outcomes is to use Census tract fixed effects to account for time-invariant unobserved local market characteristics and county-year fixed effects to account for time-varying trends at the whole market level (see [@ellickson_grieco_jue_2013] for a similar approach). We also incorporate time-varying demographic variables in the radius around each entry location. These are intended to control for local trends in population or income associated with economic shocks. We include population, median income, and level of residential rents as well as the annual growth rate in each of these variables.[^25] We also include a time-varying measure of overall business activity by constructing the total number of active businesses (excluding grocery and dollar stores) at the radius-year level, which we label "business density." Our sample includes locations (tracts) with no dollar store entry, and so we effectively compare locations with entry to those without as well as comparing treated locations who experience treatment at different times. In Appendix [5](#apx:additional_tables), we show robustness to alternative control groups, including results that exclude never-treated locations from the control group and estimate results only from differences in timing among treated locations.[^26]

We estimate effects using the following specification:

$$
\label{eq:RF_exit_main}
    Y_{lbt} =  \delta DS_{lt} + \beta \mathbf{X}_{lbt} + \lambda_l + \alpha_{mt} + \epsilon_{lbt}
$$

where $\lambda_l$ and $\alpha_{mt}$ represent location and county-time fixed effects, and the objects of interest $\delta$ are the coefficients on the number of dollar store chain entry events in location $l$ in period $t$. The outcome variable $Y_{lbt}$ is the number of independent grocery stores at distance band $b$ around the entry location $l$ in period $t$. We also define $\mathbf{X}_{lbt}$, the local demographics (including growth rates), at this level. To capture potential non-linear effects of changes in market structure, we include $DS_{lt}$ as a categorical variable.

Before estimating Equation ([\[eq:RF_exit_main\]](#eq:RF_exit_main)), we show graphical results via an event study analysis over the years before and after the dollar store chain entry occurs. In this specification, we estimate:

$$
\label{eq:RF_exit_eventstudy}
    Y_{lbt} = \sum\limits_{\tau} \delta_\tau E_{l,t-\tau} + \beta \mathbf{X}_{lbt} + \lambda_l + \alpha_{mt} + \epsilon_{lbt}
$$

where $E_{lt}$ denote a dummy for whether a dollar store entry has occurred in location $l$ by period $t$. This differs from the specification in Equation ([\[eq:RF_exit_main\]](#eq:RF_exit_main)) in that the coefficients on dollar store entry are subscripted by $\tau$, the difference in years measured relative to the entry date. This allows for both dynamic policy effects, such as a delay in the effect on local markets as the dollar store's sales ramp up, and for detecting the presence of pre-trends in grocery store activity prior to dollar store entry. For this analysis, we also focus only on a binary dummy for whether or not an entry occurs, rather than the number of entries. As before, we present results based on heterogeneity-robust estimators, e.g., [@CALLAWAY2021200]. [^27]

## Fresh Produce Spending and Grocery Store Exit

The reduction in fresh produce spending documented above may be due to a direct effect (e.g., substitution away from produce due to preferences for the dollar store format and products) and an indirect effect (e.g., reduced access to produce from changes in market structure). We provide evidence that the impact of dollar store entry on fresh produce spending acts in part through exits of grocery stores and reduced access. [\[table:reg_frshprod_decomposition\]](#table:reg_frshprod_decomposition) shows the effect of dollar store entry on fresh produce spending, controlling for the number of grocery store exits (all in the 0-2mi radius). Given the effect heterogeneity uncovered in [4](#fig:frshprod_anyds_byincome), we interact these market structure changes with household income (below or above bin $7$).



Comparing column $(1)$, which includes dollar store entries only, to column $(2)$ indicates that, for households with income in bin 7 or below, grocery store exits explain a significant proportion ($43\%$) of the effect of dollar store entry on fresh produce spending. For instance, the aggregate effect of three dollar store entries on households' spending (-\$$15.86$ in column (1)) can be decomposed into a direct effect (-\$$8.17$ in column (2)) and an indirect effect from one grocery store exit (-\$$6.82$ in column (2)).[^31]

Relative to previous research finding limited effects of entry of grocery stores and supermarkets ([@Allcott2019]), the analysis above demonstrates that $(1)$ grocery stores are driven out by dollar store entry in close proximity; $(2)$ the exit of many grocery stores is associated with a moderate but significant reduction in spending on and volumes of fresh produce. This asymmetric impact of grocery store entries and exits on healthy grocery shopping may be tied to consumer inertia in store and brand choices ([@bell_ho_tang], [@Dube2010]). Inertia in households' shopping may create asymmetries in the response to positive or negative shocks to their retail environment. That is, the loss of a frequently visited store may cause consumers to make changes to their purchase bundle in a way that the addition of a new nearby store does not.

# Conclusion {#sec:conclusion}

The rise of dollar store chains has profoundly reshaped the U.S. retail sector. By $2021$, more than $75$ percent of the U.S. population lives within five miles of a dollar store. This rapid expansion has been met with growing scrutiny from policymakers. Proponents claim that dollar stores broaden shopping options and improve convenience for consumers in underserved areas. Advocates of tighter controls argue that these chains threaten local independent stores, discourage entry by full-line grocery stores, and limit consumers' access to fresh produce. In many municipalities, controls take the form of zoning ordinances that limit dollar store density.

Despite the extensive public policy debates, there remains a need for careful empirical evidence supporting the arguments advanced. This paper brings new data and methods to bear on these questions. We quantify the impacts of dollar stores' expansion and focus on two sets of outcomes. First, we consider market structure, that is, the geographic layout and number of retail stores by format. This outcome is particularly important as it affects retail proximity, convenience, and product variety. Second, we consider consumers' shopping behavior, including their spending across retail formats and food categories (e.g., fresh produce).

Our analysis demonstrates that dollar store entry leads to a significant reduction in household spending and volumes purchased of fresh produce. We also find a significant decrease in the number of grocery stores, especially in close proximity (0--2mi) from the entry location. We find that markets lose one grocery store for every three new dollar stores. Moreover, we highlight the distributional consequences of these findings: fresh produce consumption and grocery access decrease most for consumers who initially tend to eat less healthfully and have higher transportation costs---low income, older, from minority groups, without access to a vehicle. Overall, we find that negative shocks to food availability have a meaningful impact on consumers' dietary choices. These results add nuance to the literature on the determinants of nutritional inequality.

The welfare implications of dollar store expansion are arguably multifaceted. Dollar store entry may affect consumer welfare through changes in prices (both at dollar stores and through their competitors' response), changes in store convenience (or travel costs), and changes in product availability and the ensuing composition of consumers' shopping baskets. In the medium to long-run, changes in consumers' dietary choices can have important implications for health outcomes. While the existing data and the many channels above prevent an estimation approach that accounts for all these dimensions in a single model, we view this paper as a necessary first step in quantifying this impact and informing the debate around the place of the dollar store format in the U.S. retail landscape.
